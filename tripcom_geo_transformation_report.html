Executive Summary

The Invisible Transition Already Underway

Trip.com Group occupies a paradoxical position in 2026: it is simultaneously the most AI-native major OTA on the planet — with TripGenie showing 200% traffic growth in 2024 and the company's leadership explicitly stating "AI agents will replace traditional search engines as primary entry points" — and one of the least GEO-ready brands in terms of its external content authority, structured data deployment, and citation presence in third-party AI systems. The company has built the internal AI infrastructure but has not yet engineered itself to be the cited and trusted source that external AI models (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews) will recommend when travelers ask the questions that precede a booking.

Core strategic imperative: Trip.com must shift its marketing philosophy from "win the click" to "win the cite." In a world where AI-referred sessions are growing 527% year-over-year and Gartner projects a 25% decline in traditional search volume by 2026, the question is no longer whether to pursue GEO — it is how quickly Trip.com can build the content, technical, and trust infrastructure to become the source that AI models reach for when travelers ask about Asia-Pacific routes, multi-destination itineraries, and value-first booking decisions.

01

Current-State Diagnosis

How Trip.com Markets Today

Marketing Objectives

Trip.com's current marketing model is dominated by three objectives: user acquisition (capturing first-time bookers, particularly in non-China APAC, Middle East, and emerging markets); transactional conversion (turning search intent into immediate bookings); and platform stickiness (driving app installs and repeat engagement via TripGenie and the Trip Moments UGC layer).

What is conspicuously absent is a fourth, increasingly critical objective: AI citation authority — the deliberate effort to become the source that large language models surface when travelers ask planning-stage questions. This absence is the structural vulnerability this report addresses.

Channel Mix and Estimated Allocation

Based on public disclosures and industry analysis, Trip.com allocates approximately 20% of net revenue to marketing and sales — in 2025 terms, roughly $1.6–$1.8B USD. The channel mix skews heavily toward paid acquisition and conversion-stage media.

Key observation: The channel mix is almost entirely conversion-stage. Over 60% of spend (SEM + metasearch) targets travelers who already know where they're going and are deciding where to book. This model is competitively expensive — Booking.com and Expedia have deeper loyalty moats and brand recall that lower their effective CPA. Trip.com cannot win a pure SEM arms race against Booking Holdings' $6B+ annual marketing machine.

Content Capabilities

Trip.com has built meaningful proprietary content assets — Trip.Best, Trip.Deals, Trip.Trends, and the Trip Moments UGC layer — which are well-integrated into TripGenie's internal recommendation engine. However, these assets are primarily functional (structured for booking conversion) rather than editorially authoritative (structured to be cited by external AI systems).

The company produces destination guides, ranking lists, and travel trend reports, but these lack the E-E-A-T signals (Expert authorship, Experience documentation, Authoritative sourcing, Trustworthiness indicators) that make content citable by LLMs. Trip.com's content reads as an OTA — it does not yet read as a travel knowledge authority.

Data and Analytics Capabilities

Trip.com possesses genuinely world-class proprietary data — real-time flight and hotel inventory, anonymized booking patterns, pricing trends, and traveler behavior across 60,000+ ecosystem partners. This data is Trip.com's most underexploited GEO asset. Published as structured, citable research (trend reports, pricing analyses, destination insights), it would become exactly the kind of authoritative, data-backed content that AI engines preferentially cite.

AI agents will not replace OTAs. Instead, they will complement us. They are likely to replace traditional search engines as primary entry points for traffic, opening new customer acquisition channels.

— James Liang, Executive Chairman, Trip.com Group

This is precisely correct — and it creates an urgent strategic implication: if AI replaces the front door of travel research, and Trip.com is not the source AI cites, then the company cedes its top-of-funnel to competitors who are. The company's leadership has diagnosed the shift; this report provides the activation architecture.

02

Gap Analysis

What Stands Between Trip.com and GEO Leadership

The gaps are organized across three dimensions: capability, technical, and mindset. Each dimension is assessed by severity.

Capability Gaps

CRITICAL Answer-Oriented Content Design
  • Content is optimized for click-through, not for AI extraction and citation
  • No "atomic answer" architecture — FAQs, definitions, direct-response formats
  • Destination pages are booking funnels, not knowledge resources
  • No structured Q&A layers that LLMs can lift as authoritative answers
CRITICAL External Citation & Earned Media Authority
  • Trip.com is rarely cited in Reddit, Quora, or editorial travel media — the sources LLMs trust most
  • Wikipedia presence is thin compared to Booking.com, Expedia, and Airbnb
  • No structured program for getting data/insights syndicated to press
  • AI systems exhibit strong bias toward earned media over brand-owned content
HIGH Expert Voice & Authorship Signals
  • No bylined expert contributors or destination specialists publishing under Trip.com brand
  • Content lacks visible author credentials, biographical context, or expertise signals
  • No "thought leadership" program competing with Booking.com's travel trend reports
HIGH Proprietary Data Publishing Program
  • Trip.com's booking data is a dormant citation asset — it sits in TripGenie, not in the world
  • No systematic publication of travel trend reports, pricing indices, or demand forecasts
  • Competitors (Booking, Airbnb, Expedia) regularly publish data studies that get cited by media and, subsequently, by LLMs
MEDIUM Multilingual Content Depth
  • GEO requires language-native content, not translated content — AI models assess fluency and local authority
  • English-market content depth significantly trails Booking.com and TripAdvisor
  • European language content (DE, FR, ES) is thin relative to market size
MEDIUM Community & Forum Presence
  • Trip.com is absent from the communities LLMs heavily weight: r/travel, TripAdvisor forums, Lonely Planet boards
  • Trip Moments is an in-app UGC layer — not indexed as open-web content that AI crawlers can access

Technical Gaps

CRITICAL Schema Markup & Structured Data
  • Incomplete or absent FAQPage, HowTo, TouristDestination, LodgingBusiness, and Flight schema on key landing pages
  • No Article / NewsArticle schema on editorial content — LLMs cannot properly attribute authorship
  • Knowledge Graph entity consolidation not optimized — Trip.com's brand entity is weakly connected
HIGH AI Crawlability & LLM Access
  • No llms.txt or equivalent file to guide LLM crawlers to authoritative content
  • Robots.txt likely blocking AI scrapers — needs audit and strategic allowlisting
  • Dynamic content served via JavaScript is invisible to many AI crawlers
HIGH Entity Definition & Knowledge Graph
  • Wikidata and Wikipedia entries for Trip.com lack depth, citations, and structured facts
  • Trip.Best and Trip.Trends ranking methodologies are not published or linkable
  • Brand entity does not clearly bind to recognized expertise domains in knowledge graphs
MEDIUM Content Fragmentation & Canonical Structure
  • Information is siloed across app, website, and newsroom — no unified content hub that AI can index comprehensively
  • Duplicate or near-duplicate destination content across Skyscanner and Trip.com creates canonicalization confusion

Mindset Gaps

CRITICAL Traffic Acquisition vs. Trust Acquisition
  • Marketing KPIs are entirely traffic and conversion-oriented — no measurement of citation share, AI visibility, or trust equity
  • "Win the booking" mindset excludes "win the recommendation" as a measurable objective
  • Success is defined at the bottom of the funnel; GEO requires investing at the top — with a longer attribution lag
HIGH Platform Thinking vs. Publisher Thinking
  • Trip.com thinks like a marketplace — it aggregates supply and demand — not like a knowledge publisher that earns external citations
  • The editorial layer (Trip.Best rankings, trend data) is treated as a product feature, not as a public authority asset
HIGH China-Brand Asymmetry
  • Trip.com's parent brand (Ctrip) is the dominant China platform — but this association creates friction in Western markets where brand provenance is a trust variable for AI models
  • No explicit strategy to build Western-market credibility as a distinct brand entity
MEDIUM Attribution Myopia
  • Last-click attribution models do not capture the value of an AI citation that influences brand consideration 30 days before a booking
  • Long-horizon trust investments appear as cost centers in current measurement models

Transformation Roadmap

Three Horizons to GEO Leadership

The roadmap is structured across three horizons: Foundation (0–6 months), Acceleration (6–18 months), and Authority (18–36 months). Each horizon builds on the last and requires distinct organizational capabilities.

H1
Horizon 1 — Foundation · 0–6 months

Fix the Floor: Technical & Content Groundwork

The first horizon is not glamorous — it is the structural work that makes GEO possible. No content strategy works without the technical infrastructure that allows AI systems to discover, trust, and cite Trip.com's content. Simultaneously, the measurement architecture must be established to make future investments defensible.

  • TechnicalSchema markup audit and deployment across all destination, hotel, and flight pages (TouristDestination, LodgingBusiness, FAQPage, Flight)
  • TechnicalRobots.txt and llms.txt strategic audit — allowlist reputable AI crawlers (GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot) for designated content sections
  • TechnicalConsolidate Skyscanner and Trip.com canonical content — eliminate duplicate destination pages creating LLM confusion
  • TechnicalWikidata/Wikipedia entity enrichment program — establish Trip.com, Trip.Best, and TripGenie as well-defined entities with sourced facts
  • ContentLaunch "Answer Architecture" content templates — restructure top 200 destination/route pages with direct-answer introductions (40–60 words), FAQ layers, and citable statistics
  • ContentEstablish Trip.com Travel Intelligence Hub — a public-facing content destination for data-driven travel insights and research
  • MeasurementDeploy GEO monitoring stack: track brand mentions in ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews, Claude across 500 priority travel queries
  • MeasurementEstablish AI-referred session tracking in analytics — create a distinct "AI referral" channel bucket separate from organic
H2
Horizon 2 — Acceleration · 6–18 months

Build the Authority Layer: Content, Data, and Earned Media

With the technical foundation in place, Horizon 2 focuses on the content and credibility investments that determine Trip.com's citation frequency in AI-generated responses. This is where Trip.com's proprietary data advantage becomes deployable as a GEO asset.

  • Data PublishingLaunch quarterly "Trip.com Global Travel Index" — a data-driven report on booking trends, price movements, and emerging destinations, distributed to press and indexed for AI citation
  • Data PublishingPublish destination-level pricing transparency reports (e.g., "Cost of a Week in Tokyo: 2026 Data") — high LLM citation probability for planning-stage queries
  • Expert ContentBuild a network of 30–50 bylined destination specialists and travel writers publishing under the Trip.com brand — author schema with verifiable credentials
  • Expert ContentCommission academic-style deep-dive reports on travel behavior, affordability, and cultural tourism — designed explicitly for press pickup and LLM citation
  • Earned MediaEstablish a PR-to-GEO pipeline: every data release includes structured press materials, embeddable statistics, and citation-ready formats
  • Earned MediaActively place Trip.com experts in Reuters, BBC Travel, The Guardian, Forbes Travel — building the editorial citation trail that LLMs weight
  • CommunityStrategic seeding program for Reddit (r/travel, r/solotravel, r/digitalnomad) and Tripadvisor forums — non-promotional, genuinely useful content from Trip.com staff and community managers
  • ProductMake Trip Moments open-web indexable — transform in-app UGC into indexable travel content pages with proper schema and author attribution
H3
Horizon 3 — Authority · 18–36 months

Own the Category: Become the Cited Source for AI-Era Travel

By Horizon 3, Trip.com should have transitioned from a search-arbitrage OTA to a recognized travel intelligence authority — a brand that AI models reach for by default when travelers ask questions that precede a booking decision. This horizon is about compounding the authority built in H1–H2 and extending it into new formats and markets.

  • AuthorityTrip.com Travel Institute — a permanent, branded research and intelligence function publishing peer-reviewed-style travel economics and cultural insights
  • AuthorityAnnual "State of Global Travel" report — anchoring Trip.com as the definitive voice on travel trends, comparable to Airbnb's design reports or Booking.com's sustainable travel studies
  • ProductGEO-native content API — structured data feeds that allow media, academics, and AI systems to access Trip.com's travel intelligence in machine-readable formats
  • BrandWestern-market brand architecture — establish Trip.com as a distinct, credible, Western-friendly brand with its own editorial identity separate from Ctrip's China positioning
  • MeasurementAI Share of Voice becomes a primary marketing KPI alongside revenue — with quarterly board reporting on citation frequency across major LLMs
  • ExpansionGEO localization playbooks for priority markets: UK, Germany, India, Australia, UAE — each with native-language content authority programs
  • EcosystemPartner ecosystem for co-citation: co-publish research with tourism boards, airlines, and hospitality chains — multiplying citation surfaces
  • InnovationDevelop Trip.com-native AI content tools that help partners structure their content for GEO — positioning Trip.com as the AI marketing infrastructure for the travel ecosystem

Implementation Recommendations

Organizational Structure, Workflow, and Tooling

Organizational Structure

Core recommendation: Create a dedicated GEO & Content Authority function sitting within Marketing but with a direct mandate from the Chief Marketing Officer. This team is distinct from SEO (which is conversion-stage) and from product content (which serves TripGenie). Its sole KPI is Trip.com's citation presence in external AI systems.

GEO & Content Authority Team (New)

  • Head of GEO (Director-level, reports to CMO)
  • Content architects: 3–4 specialists in answer-oriented content design
  • Data journalism unit: 2 analysts converting booking data to publishable insights
  • Technical GEO: 1–2 engineers for schema, crawlability, and structured data
  • AI monitoring: 1 analyst tracking citation share across platforms

Editorial & PR-to-GEO Bridge (Expanded)

  • Expand existing PR function with a "citation architecture" brief
  • Every data asset must be produced with an LLM-citation brief alongside the press brief
  • Monthly cross-functional GEO council: Marketing, Product, Data Science, Legal
  • Bylined contributor network management (destination specialists)

Existing SEO Team (Reoriented)

  • Shift 30% of SEO team bandwidth to GEO-aligned technical work: schema, entity optimization, crawl access
  • SEO and GEO share a unified "Content Authority" P&L — eliminating competition for budget
  • SEO team upskilling in structured data and AI visibility measurement

Product & TripGenie Integration

  • Ensure Trip Moments UGC is architectured for open-web indexability from inception
  • TripGenie's internal content assets (Trip.Best, Trip.Trends) must have public-facing canonical URLs optimized for LLM citation
  • Collaborate with data science to build the pipeline from internal data to publishable insights

Workflow Design

The most important workflow change is the introduction of a "Dual Brief" model for all significant content assets. Every content piece is briefed twice: once for traditional marketing objectives (traffic, conversion) and once for GEO objectives (citation architecture, fact density, authority signals). This prevents GEO from becoming an afterthought appended to existing processes.

Content Production Workflow

  • Query mining: Before writing, identify 5–10 AI-likely questions the content should answer definitively
  • Answer-first drafting: Lead every section with a 40–60 word direct answer, then expand
  • Fact density check: Minimum 1 verifiable statistic per 150 words, with source attribution
  • Schema tagging: Schema markup applied during publishing, not as a post-production step
  • Citation seeding: Each published asset has a structured outreach plan for press and community pickup

Data-to-Insight Pipeline

  • Monthly data pull: Data science team provides a GEO team "insight pack" — top booking trends, price anomalies, emerging routes
  • Curation layer: Data journalism unit converts raw data into press-ready insights with framing, context, and visualization
  • Publication cadence: Weekly micro-insights (social/blog), monthly data notes, quarterly major reports
  • Distribution: Reuters Connect, PR Newswire, sector-specific databases — ensuring LLM training data inclusion

Attribution Model Evolution

Trip.com must move from last-click to an influence-weighted attribution model that captures the role of AI citations in brand consideration. The practical implementation involves:

Short-term (H1)

  • Tag and segment all AI-referred sessions in GA4 with a distinct source/medium group
  • Track brand search lift in weeks following major AI citation events
  • Survey 5% of new bookings: "How did you first hear of Trip.com?" — capture AI assistant mentions

Medium-term (H2–H3)

  • Build a Marketing Mix Modeling (MMM) layer that includes AI citation events as an input variable
  • Pilot controlled studies: vary GEO content investment by market, measure downstream booking lift
  • Report "Trust Equity Score" quarterly — a composite of citation share, brand search, and NPS that boards can understand

The strategic framing for leadership: GEO is not a marketing experiment — it is insurance against search disintermediation. AI-referred traffic converts at 4.4× the rate of standard organic search. A 20% share of AI citations in Trip.com's core query categories could deliver $400–700M in incremental attributed revenue annually at scale — without a single incremental dollar of paid acquisition spend. The question is not whether to invest in GEO. It is how fast to move before Booking.com and Expedia capture the AI citation landscape permanently.

The Compounding Advantage

Unlike paid search, which requires continuous spend to maintain visibility, GEO investments compound. A high-quality, well-structured article that becomes a cited source for AI systems continues to generate citations and referrals for years. Trip.com's proprietary data assets — if published systematically — create a citation flywheel: data generates press coverage, press coverage generates LLM training citations, LLM citations generate awareness and bookings, bookings generate more data. This is the trust economy in its most powerful form.

The brands that win the AI era will not be those with the largest paid search budgets. They will be the brands that AI systems have learned to trust — because they published the facts, cited the sources, and showed their work.
